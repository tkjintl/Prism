● Theme 01  /  Secondary Batteries

The dip is noise.
The trend is structural.

The current numbers are uncomfortable — LGES revenue fell 24% in 2024, Korean Big 3 are running at 40–50% utilisation versus CATL and BYD at 90%. This is the dip. It is caused by a specific, temporary condition: Chinese oversupply created a price war that compressed near-term demand and customer ordering behaviour. It is not a structural reversal of the EV transition.

The recovery signals are already visible. Global EV sales exceeded 17 million units in 2024 — over 20% of new car sales for the first time. LGES signed a $4.3B LFP cell deal with Tesla Energy. ● Theme 02  /  Energy Storage

Storage is the
infrastructure of
the energy transition.

Renewable energy is intermittent. The grid needs to balance supply and demand in real time. Energy storage is the missing layer — and the IRA has fundamentally changed its economics.

In 2024, global battery storage installations hit 168–173 GWh — a 75% jump year-on-year. 2025 is forecast at 247 GWh. By 2030, BloombergNEF projects global storage to be 15 times larger than 2019 levels.

The US IRA's standalone storage Investment Tax Credit (30–70% of capex) is a structural demand floor that makes ESS projects financeable regardless of wholesale power prices. LG Energy Solution signed a $4.3B LFP cell deal with Tesla Energy specifically to serve this market through 2030.

● Theme 03  /  Electricity Infrastructure

The grid is failing.
Demand is
accelerating.

The US power grid was built for a different world. Most of it is approaching or past its design life. Simultaneously, AI data centres, EVs, and electrification are adding demand at a pace the grid was never designed to handle.

The CISA formally designated the power transformer shortage a critical infrastructure threat in June 2024. Lead times for large transformers have hit 4 years. Prices are up 95% since 2020. There is no short-term domestic solution — the US simply does not manufacture enough.

US utilities have responded by accelerating import sourcing — and Korea, already the #2 supplier after China, is the primary beneficiary of de-risking policy.

Risk factors
& how we think
about them.

Strong conviction in a thesis does not mean dismissing its risks. We identify four material risks — and the structural mitigants already embedded in our approach.

⏱
EV Demand Timing Uncertainty

The battery supercycle depends on US and European EV adoption accelerating. If the transition takes longer than anticipated, equipment order volumes stay depressed.

✓ Mitigant: Our exposure is at the vendor/equipment layer, not OEM. Equipment vendors supply all three Korean OEMs — no single-customer concentration. Materials investments (ENCHEM, ANP) are less cyclical than equipment.
🇨🇳
Chinese Competition

CATL holds 37.9% and BYD 17.2% of the 2024 global EV battery market — combined 55%. Korean Big 3 hold ~19% combined at 40–50% utilisation. Chinese equipment makers (Yinghe Technology) are also expanding. This is the real competitive picture.

✓ Mitigant: CATL and BYD are explicitly excluded from IRA-qualifying supply chains. US FEOC rules bar Chinese battery components from tax-credit-eligible vehicles from 2025. Korean exporters are price-accepted on grid hardware — the transformer shortage means buyers have no alternative. The policy moat is structural, not temporary.
💱
KRW / USD FX Exposure

Portfolio companies are KRW-denominated. International LPs receive returns in USD. Currency movements can meaningfully affect realised returns.

✓ Mitigant: Most portfolio companies generate significant USD/EUR export revenue — natural hedge at the operating level. Fund-level FX hedging approach to be discussed with LPs on a case-by-case basis.

Key-Person Concentration
